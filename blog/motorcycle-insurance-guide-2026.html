Motorcycle Insurance Guide 2026: What You Need to Know

Motorcycle insurance is legally required in 48 of 50 US states, and even in the two that do not require it -- Florida and Montana -- riding without insurance is a financial gamble that no responsible rider should take. A single at-fault accident without coverage can result in personal liability for hundreds of thousands of dollars in medical bills, property damage, and legal fees. Insurance is not optional. It is the cost of responsible riding.

But the motorcycle insurance market is confusing by design. Insurers profit when you do not understand your coverage, when you overpay for protection you do not need, or when you underpay and discover the gap after a crash. This guide explains every type of motorcycle coverage in plain language, provides real cost data for 2026, and shows you exactly how to build a policy that protects you without wasting money.

Liability Coverage Explained

Liability coverage is the foundation of every motorcycle insurance policy and the only coverage legally required in most states. It pays for injuries and property damage you cause to other people when you are at fault in an accident. It does not pay for your own injuries or damage to your own motorcycle.

Bodily Injury Liability

Bodily injury liability covers medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and legal defense costs when you injure someone else in an accident you caused. Policies list two limits: per-person and per-accident. A 50/100 policy means up to $50,000 per person and $100,000 per accident. The minimum required limits vary by state, ranging from 10/20 in some states to 50/100 in others. However, minimum limits are dangerously low. A single serious injury can easily exceed $100,000 in medical bills. Carrying 100/300 limits costs only $50 to $150 more per year than minimum limits and provides vastly better protection.

Property Damage Liability

Property damage liability pays for damage you cause to someone else's property, including their vehicle, structures like fences or guardrails, and other property. The average property damage claim in 2025 was approximately $5,700, but hitting a luxury vehicle or causing a multi-car pileup can result in claims exceeding $50,000. Most states require $10,000 to $25,000 in property damage liability, but carrying $50,000 or more is recommended.

Collision Coverage

Collision coverage pays to repair or replace your motorcycle when it is damaged in an accident, regardless of who is at fault. If you are at fault, collision coverage is the only way to get your bike repaired. If the other driver is at fault, your collision coverage pays upfront and your insurer pursues the other driver's insurance for reimbursement.

When You Need It

Collision coverage is essential if your motorcycle is worth more than $3,000 to $5,000, if you are still making loan payments (your lender will require it), or if you could not afford to replace your bike out of pocket. It is less necessary for older, lower-value bikes where the annual premium might approach the bike's actual value.

Deductibles

Collision coverage comes with a deductible, the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Common deductibles are $250, $500, and $1,000. A higher deductible lowers your premium but increases your out-of-pocket cost in a claim. For most riders, a $500 deductible provides the best balance. Going from a $500 to a $1,000 deductible typically saves only $50 to $100 per year, which means it takes 5 to 10 years of premium savings to offset the additional $500 you would pay in a claim.

Comprehensive Coverage

Comprehensive coverage pays for damage to your motorcycle from events other than collisions. This includes theft, vandalism, fire, weather damage (hail, flooding, falling trees), hitting an animal, and damage from civil unrest. If your motorcycle is stolen, comprehensive coverage pays the actual cash value of the bike minus your deductible.

Why It Matters for Motorcycles

Motorcycles are stolen at roughly twice the rate of cars relative to their numbers on the road. The National Insurance Crime Bureau reports that over 52,000 motorcycles were reported stolen in 2024 in the United States. Honda, Yamaha, and Kawasaki are the most frequently stolen brands. Comprehensive coverage is relatively inexpensive, typically adding $50 to $200 per year to your policy, and is strongly recommended for any motorcycle worth more than $2,000.

Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Uninsured motorist coverage protects you when you are hit by a driver who has no insurance. Underinsured motorist coverage protects you when the at-fault driver's insurance is insufficient to cover your injuries and damages. The Insurance Research Council estimates that approximately 14% of drivers nationwide are uninsured, with rates exceeding 25% in some states.

Why This Is Critical for Riders

Motorcyclists are far more vulnerable in collisions than car occupants. When a car hits you, your injuries are likely to be severe: broken bones, road rash, head injuries, and spinal injuries are common even in moderate-speed impacts. If the driver who hits you has no insurance or minimal coverage, your medical bills could reach six figures with no one to pay them. Uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage is arguably the most important coverage after liability for motorcycle riders. It is your safety net when the person who hit you cannot pay.

Medical Payments and Personal Injury Protection

Medical payments coverage pays your medical bills after a motorcycle accident regardless of fault, up to the policy limit. It covers hospital bills, surgery, rehabilitation, and sometimes funeral expenses. Limits typically range from $5,000 to $25,000. Personal Injury Protection is similar but broader, covering lost wages and essential services in addition to medical bills. PIP is required in no-fault states like Michigan, New Jersey, and New York.

How It Works with Health Insurance

Medical payments coverage works as a supplement to your health insurance. It can pay your health insurance deductible, cover copays, and pay for medical expenses your health plan does not cover. If you have a high-deductible health plan, medical payments coverage is particularly valuable because it prevents a motorcycle accident from also triggering thousands of dollars in health insurance deductibles.

Optional Coverages Worth Considering

  • Accessory Coverage: Standard policies cover your motorcycle at its factory value. If you have added aftermarket exhaust, custom paint, suspension upgrades, or other modifications, accessory coverage protects that additional investment. Coverage typically ranges from $1,000 to $30,000 in additional value.
  • Roadside Assistance: Motorcycle-specific roadside assistance covers towing (motorcycles require flatbed towing, which costs $150 to $300 per tow), flat tire service, battery jump, and lockout service. This typically costs $15 to $30 per year and is worth it for any rider who covers significant miles.
  • Rental Reimbursement: If your motorcycle is your primary transportation, rental reimbursement covers a rental vehicle while your bike is being repaired after a covered claim. This costs approximately $10 to $20 per year.
  • Trip Interruption: For touring riders, trip interruption coverage pays for lodging, meals, and transportation if your motorcycle breaks down or is involved in an accident more than a specified distance from home, typically 100 miles.
  • Agreed Value vs. Actual Cash Value: Standard policies pay actual cash value (market value minus depreciation) if your bike is totaled. Agreed value policies pay a pre-determined amount that you and the insurer agree upon when the policy is written. Agreed value is essential for classic, rare, or heavily customized motorcycles whose market value exceeds their depreciated value.

Factors That Affect Your Premium

Insurance companies use dozens of data points to calculate your premium. Understanding these factors helps you predict your costs and take actions that lower them.

  • Age and Experience: Riders under 25 pay 50 to 100 percent more than riders over 30. Experience matters more than age. A 30-year-old with one year of riding experience pays more than a 30-year-old with ten years.
  • Driving Record: A clean record for three or more years earns the best rates. A single at-fault accident can increase your premium 20 to 40 percent. A DUI or reckless driving conviction can double or triple your rate.
  • Credit Score: In states where it is allowed, your credit score is a significant rating factor. Riders with excellent credit pay 30 to 50 percent less than riders with poor credit for the same coverage.
  • Location: Urban areas have higher premiums than rural areas due to increased theft risk, traffic density, and higher medical costs. Your specific ZIP code matters more than your state average.
  • Annual Mileage: Riders who log fewer miles per year pay less. A rider who commutes daily on their motorcycle pays more than a weekend-only rider because more miles means more exposure to risk.
  • The Motorcycle: Engine displacement, horsepower, and the bike's claim history all affect your rate. A 300cc beginner bike costs far less to insure than a 1000cc sport bike.

How to Lower Your Motorcycle Insurance Cost

Bundle Policies

Combining your motorcycle insurance with your auto, renters, or homeowners insurance with the same company typically saves 10 to 25 percent on all policies. This is consistently the single largest discount available.

Complete a Safety Course

The MSF Basic RiderCourse or equivalent state-approved course earns a 5 to 15 percent discount from most insurers. Some companies also discount for advanced riding courses like Total Control or track day instruction programs.

Increase Your Deductible

Raising your collision deductible from $250 to $500 typically saves 15 to 25 percent on the collision portion of your premium. Going from $500 to $1,000 saves another 10 to 15 percent. Only increase your deductible to an amount you can comfortably pay out of pocket.

Install Anti-Theft Devices

A disc lock, chain lock, GPS tracker, or alarm system can earn a 5 to 10 percent discount on comprehensive coverage. Some insurers require specific anti-theft devices. LoJack and similar GPS recovery systems often qualify for the largest discounts.

Seasonal Storage Discount

If you store your motorcycle during winter months, ask about seasonal or lay-up coverage that reduces your premium by suspending liability and collision coverage while maintaining comprehensive protection. This can save 30 to 50 percent during storage months.

Shop Around Every Year

Insurance rates change annually as companies adjust their risk models. The cheapest insurer last year may not be the cheapest this year. Get quotes from at least three to five companies every renewal period. Progressive, GEICO, Dairyland, Foremost, and Markel are the major motorcycle insurance specialists worth comparing.

Best Motorcycle Insurance Providers 2026

Progressive -- Best Overall

Progressive insures more motorcycles than any other company in the United States and offers the widest range of coverage options. Their online quote tool provides side-by-side rate comparisons from multiple insurers, making them a useful starting point even if you ultimately choose a competitor. Accessory coverage up to $30,000, OEM parts coverage, and carried contents coverage are available. Their roadside assistance program includes motorcycle-specific flatbed towing.

GEICO -- Best for Bundling

GEICO provides significant multi-policy discounts when you bundle motorcycle coverage with auto or renters insurance. Their rates are consistently competitive, particularly for riders with clean records and good credit. The online management tools are excellent, and the claims process is straightforward. GEICO does not offer as many motorcycle-specific coverage add-ons as Progressive, but their core coverage and pricing are strong.

Dairyland -- Best for High-Risk Riders

Dairyland specializes in insuring riders that other companies turn away or charge excessive rates: young riders, riders with accidents or violations on their records, and riders with lapsed coverage. Their rates for high-risk profiles are typically 20 to 40 percent lower than standard insurers. Coverage options are more limited than Progressive or GEICO, but for riders who struggle to find affordable coverage elsewhere, Dairyland is often the answer.

Common Insurance Mistakes

  • Carrying only minimum liability: State minimums of 10/20/10 or 25/50/25 are dangerously insufficient. One serious injury claim can exhaust minimum limits and leave you personally liable for the balance. Carry at least 100/300/50.
  • Skipping uninsured motorist coverage: With 14 percent of drivers uninsured nationally and higher rates in many states, skipping UM coverage is gambling that every driver who hits you will have adequate insurance.
  • Not disclosing modifications: Aftermarket modifications that are not disclosed to your insurer may not be covered in a claim. If your exhaust, suspension, or other mods increase your bike's value, add accessory coverage.
  • Letting coverage lapse: A gap in insurance coverage, even for a few days, can result in significantly higher rates when you reinstate coverage. If you are not riding, switch to a storage policy rather than canceling entirely.
  • Not reviewing coverage annually: Your bike depreciates, your riding habits change, and insurance rates fluctuate. Review your coverage limits and deductibles at every renewal to ensure you are not overpaying for more coverage than your bike's current value justifies.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does motorcycle insurance cost in 2026?

The average motorcycle insurance cost in 2026 is $700 to $1,500 per year for full coverage, or $200 to $500 per year for liability-only. Your actual cost depends on your age, riding experience, location, the motorcycle you ride, your driving record, and the coverage levels you choose. Sport bikes cost 50 to 100 percent more to insure than cruisers. Riders under 25 pay significantly higher premiums.

What motorcycle insurance coverage do I need?

At minimum, you need liability coverage, which is required by law in 48 states. Beyond the legal minimum, most riders should carry uninsured motorist coverage, medical payments or personal injury protection, and comprehensive and collision if your bike is worth more than $3,000. Collision covers crash damage to your bike. Comprehensive covers theft, vandalism, weather, and animal strikes.

Does motorcycle insurance cover passengers?

Your liability coverage extends to injuries your passenger causes to others, and your medical payments or PIP coverage typically covers your passenger's medical bills up to the policy limit. However, your collision and comprehensive coverage only covers the motorcycle itself, not passenger belongings. Passengers should understand that their recovery options may be limited if you are at fault.

Can I insure a motorcycle I don't ride year-round?

Yes. Most insurers offer seasonal or lay-up policies that reduce your premium during months when your motorcycle is stored. A lay-up policy typically maintains comprehensive coverage while suspending liability and collision coverage. This can save 30 to 50 percent on your annual premium.

Does completing a motorcycle safety course lower insurance?

Yes. Most insurance companies offer a 5 to 15 percent discount for completing an approved motorcycle safety course such as the MSF Basic RiderCourse. Some states mandate this discount by law. The discount typically lasts 3 to 5 years before you need to retake the course. The insurance savings alone often pay for the course within the first year.
